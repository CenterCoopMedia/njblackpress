# Afro-American Festival of the Arts Magazine

Publication name: Afro-American Festival of the Arts Magazine
Alternate names: Anthology of Our Black Selves; Afro-Arts Anthology: Poetry, Short Stories, Plays (1969 reprint title)
Location (City/Town): Newark, New Jersey (cataloged by Brown as [Newark? N.J.])
Founder(s): Unknown
Publisher(s): Jihad
Key staff (Editors, Journalists): Editor: Yusef Iman; contributors included LeRoi Jones (Amiri Baraka), Ed Spriggs, Sonia Sanchez, Larry Neal, Joe White, Yusef Inman, and Ben Caldwell
Year founded: 1966
Year ceased: 1966
Frequency: Single known issue (August 1966)
Medium/Distribution method: Print
Format: Magazine / literary anthology
Languages: English
Primary focus/content: Arts and culture; Black Arts Movement; poetry; short fiction; plays; literary criticism
Mission statement or editorial philosophy: No formal mission statement located; the issue functioned as a Black Arts forum for showcasing Black writing, performance, and cultural criticism.
Historical notes & impact: Published in Newark in August 1966, the magazine appeared during LeRoi Jones's first years in Newark, soon after the founding of Spirit House and at an early moment in the Black Arts Movement. George Mason University identifies the volume as edited by Yusef Iman and notes the alternate title "Anthology of Our Black Selves," with a 1969 reprint as "Afro-Arts Anthology: Poetry, Short Stories, Plays." Brown University's Baraka exhibit identifies the August 1966 issue as containing work by LeRoi Jones, Ed Spriggs, Sonia Sanchez, Larry Neal, Joe White, Yusef Inman, Ben Caldwell, and others. The magazine served Black writers, artists, and Newark-area readers interested in radical Black cultural production, helping document an early network of contributors who would become central to the Black Arts Movement. Its known contents span poetry, short fiction, dramatic writing, and literary-political commentary. No stable commercial model is documented in the reviewed sources; given its small scale and movement context, it was likely sustained through direct sales, festival/event circulation, and community-based support rather than significant advertising revenue. Only a single 1966 issue is clearly documented under this title, suggesting a short run or one-off publication.
