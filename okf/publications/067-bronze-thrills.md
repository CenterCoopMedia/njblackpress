## Mission statement

Bronze Thrills provided African American readers with romance, true confessions, and women's interest stories written in a style familiar to its audience. Its predecessor, The World's Messenger (founded 1942), was innovative in featuring stories written in the dialect of Southern working-class African Americans.

## Key staff

Previous editor(s): Adelle Jackson; Edna K. Turner, Jan 1955; Eunice Wilson.

## Historical notes

Bronze Thrills was a Black romance and confessions-style magazine that evolved from The World's Messenger, founded in 1942 by Horace J. Blackwell, an African American minister and clothing merchant in Fort Worth, Texas. After Blackwell's death in 1949, his widow sold the publication to George T. Levitan, a white businessman, who renamed it Bronze Thrills and continued publishing through Good Publishing Company (also known as Sepia Publishing), based in Fort Worth. Good Publishing also produced the magazines Sepia, Hep, and Jive for African American audiences. Bronze Thrills featured true romance, true confessions, and women's interest articles alongside essays on African American issues. While Sepia did not equal the newsstand sales of Bronze Thrills, it was Sepia that became the most successful competitor to Ebony for nearly four decades. After Levitan's death in 1976, the publications were sold to Beatrice Pringle, returning them to Black ownership. The business closed around 1983. Note: All available sources identify Fort Worth, Texas as the base of Good Publishing. The connection to Dover, New Jersey may reflect a regional editorial or distribution office, but this could not be confirmed through online sources. The magazine's records and building were mostly destroyed after the business closed; the African American Museum in Dallas now holds the picture files.
