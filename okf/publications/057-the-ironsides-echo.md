## Historical notes

Student newspaper of the Manual Training and Industrial School for Colored Youth at Bordentown (the 'Bordentown School'), also known as 'the Tuskegee of the North.' The school was founded in 1886 (originally 1876) by Rev. Walter A. Rice, a former slave and AME Church minister, beginning with 10 students in two wood-clapboard homes on West Street. The name 'Ironsides' derives from the school's location on the former estate of Commodore Charles Stewart, commander of the USS Constitution ('Old Ironsides') during the War of 1812. Frances O. Grant founded the drama club and The Ironsides Echo, which won multiple prizes from organizations including the National Scholastic Association. Grant invited prominent figures including Mary McLeod Bethune, W.E.B. Du Bois, James Weldon Johnson, Paul Robeson, Albert Einstein, and Eleanor Roosevelt to speak at the school. The paper was printed by students in the printing trades program and served as outreach -- both white and Black schools exchanged papers with them. The front page of the May-June 1939 edition announced Mary McLeod Bethune as Commencement Speaker. William R. Valentine became principal in 1915, viewing the school as offering practical education, stating: 'We cannot all be doctors, lawyers, dentists, and preachers... Thus the need of men in the trades.' The school closed in 1955 following the Brown v. Board of Education decision. Digitized issues (from 1935 onward) available through the New Jersey State Library's DSpace repository.
